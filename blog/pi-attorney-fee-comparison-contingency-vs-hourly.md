## Contingency Fees Explained (The Standard PI Model)

### What Is a Contingency Fee?

**You pay nothing upfront.** The attorney gets paid a percentage of your settlement or verdict, only if you win.

### Standard Contingency Fee Percentages

| Stage of Case | Typical Fee % | What You Keep |
|--------------|---------------|---------------|
| Settlement before lawsuit | 33.33% | 66.67% |
| Settlement after lawsuit filed | 40% | 60% |
| Settlement during trial | 40-45% | 55-60% |
| Appeal to higher court | 45-50% | 50-55% |

**Example:**
- Settlement: $100,000
- Attorney fee (33.33%): $33,333
- **You net: $66,667**

### Why Contingency Fees Are Good for Injury Victims

**1. Zero Upfront Risk**
You don't pay a retainer. If you lose, you owe nothing (except possibly case costs — more on that below).

**2. Aligned Incentives**
Your lawyer only gets paid if you get paid. They're motivated to maximize your settlement.

**3. Access to Justice**
You can hire a top attorney even if you're broke. The playing field is leveled.

**4. Predictable Costs**
You know exactly what percentage you'll pay. No surprise bills.

## Hourly Fees: Why They Almost Never Make Sense for PI Cases

Some attorneys charge by the hour instead of contingency. Here's why that's usually a terrible deal for injury victims:

### Standard Hourly Rates for PI Attorneys

| Attorney Experience | Hourly Rate |
|--------------------|-------------|
| Junior associate (1-3 years) | $200-$350 |
| Mid-level associate (4-7 years) | $350-$500 |
| Senior attorney (8+ years) | $500-$700 |
| Partner/Trial lawyer | $600-$1,000+ |

### The Math That Kills Hourly Billing

**Example case:**
- Your settlement: $100,000
- Attorney hours: 50 (modest estimate)
- Rate: $500/hour
- **Total attorney fees: $25,000**

Looks good, right? You'd pay less than the 33.33% contingency fee.

**But here's the catch:**

**Real attorney hours for a $100,000 settlement case:**
- Initial consultation & case evaluation: 2 hours
- Medical record review & organization: 8 hours
- Demand letter preparation: 5 hours
- Negotiations (phone calls, emails): 12 hours
- Legal research: 6 hours
- Client communication: 8 hours
- Discovery responses: 15 hours
- Depositions: 12 hours
- Trial prep (if needed): 40+ hours

**Total: 108+ hours × $500 = $54,000+**

**You'd pay MORE with hourly billing than contingency.**

Plus, hourly attorneys often require a **retainer** (upfront payment) of $10,000-$25,000. Most injury victims don't have that cash available.

### The Only Time Hourly Makes Sense

**When you have a slam-dunk case that will settle quickly:**
- Clear liability (e.g., rear-end collision)
- Modest damages (under $50,000)
- Insurance company makes fair offer immediately

**Example:**
- Settlement: $40,000
- Attorney hours: 10 (just sends a demand letter)
- Hourly rate: $400
- **Total fee: $4,000 (10% of settlement)**

In this rare scenario, hourly saves you money versus a 33.33% contingency fee ($13,333).

**But:** Good luck finding an attorney willing to take a simple case on hourly. Most will only accept contingency because they know their time investment will exceed the hourly revenue.

## Hidden Costs: What Contingency Agreements Don't Tell You

### Case Costs vs. Attorney Fees

**Attorney fees** = what the lawyer charges for their time

**Case costs** = expenses incurred to work your case

**Critical distinction:** Even with a contingency fee, you're often responsible for case costs.

### Common Case Costs

| Expense | Cost Range |
|---------|-----------|
| Medical records | $50-$500 |
| Police reports | $25-$100 |
| Court filing fees | $300-$500 |
| Deposition transcripts | $500-$1,500 per deposition |
| Expert witness fees | $2,000-$10,000+ |
| Investigators | $1,000-$5,000 |
| Trial exhibits & demonstrative evidence | $1,000-$5,000 |

**Total case costs:** $5,000-$30,000+ for a litigated case

### How Case Costs Are Handled

**Option 1: Attorney Advances Costs (Most Common)**
The attorney pays costs upfront, then deducts them from your settlement.

**Example:**
- Settlement: $100,000
- Attorney fee (33.33%): $33,333
- Case costs (advanced by attorney): $8,000
- **You net: $58,667**

**Option 2: You Pay Costs (Less Common)**
You reimburse the attorney for costs as they're incurred. If you lose, you still owe the costs (but not the attorney fee).

**Read your contingency agreement carefully.** Some agreements deduct costs *before* calculating the attorney fee, others deduct costs *after*.

### Example Showing the Difference

**Settlement: $100,000 | Attorney Fee: 33.33% | Case Costs: $10,000**

**Method 1: Costs deducted first**
- Settlement: $100,000
- Minus costs: $10,000
- = $90,000
- Attorney fee (33.33% of $90,000): $30,000
- **You net: $60,000**

**Method 2: Costs deducted after**
- Settlement: $100,000
- Attorney fee (33.33% of $100,000): $33,333
- Minus costs: $10,000
- **You net: $56,667**

**$3,333 difference** based on one contract clause.

## Negotiating Attorney Fees: Yes, You Can

**Most attorneys are flexible on fees.** Here's when you have negotiating power:

### When You Can Negotiate:

**1. Large Case Value**
If your case is worth $500,000+, attorneys will accept a lower percentage because their dollar payout is still huge.

**Suggested negotiation:**
- 33.33% on first $100,000
- 25% on next $400,000
- 20% on amounts above $500,000

**2. Clear Liability & Quick Settlement**
If your case will settle fast without litigation, negotiate a lower fee.

**Example offer:** "I'll pay 25% if we settle within 90 days without filing suit."

**3. Referral from Another Attorney**
If another lawyer refers you, the referring attorney takes a cut (usually 33% of the fee). Use this as negotiating leverage.

**Example:** "Since you're splitting the fee with the referral attorney, can you reduce my fee to 25%?"

### Questions to Ask Before Signing

1. **What percentage do you charge if the case settles before litigation? After filing suit?**
2. **Are case costs deducted before or after calculating your fee?**
3. **Do I owe case costs if we lose?**
4. **Will you negotiate my medical liens?**
5. **What's the average case value you've obtained for injuries like mine?**

## Flat Fee Arrangements: The Future of PI Law?

Some newer firms are experimenting with **flat fee structures**:

### How It Works
- Simple case: $5,000 flat fee
- Moderate case: $15,000 flat fee
- Complex litigation: $35,000 flat fee

### Pros:
- Predictable costs
- Potentially cheaper than contingency if your settlement is high

### Cons:
- Attorney isn't incentivized to maximize your settlement
- Hard to predict case complexity upfront
- Few attorneys offer this

**Verdict:** Flat fees are rare and unlikely to replace contingency in PI cases.

## Should You Handle Your Case Without an Attorney?

### When DIY Makes Sense:
- **Minor soft tissue injury** (full recovery)
- **Clear liability** (rear-end collision, police report supports you)
- **Medical bills under $5,000**
- **No lost wages**
- **Insurance company makes reasonable offer** (compare to CaseVault data)

### When You NEED an Attorney:
- **Serious injury** (fractures, surgery, permanent disability)
- **Medical bills over $15,000**
- **Disputed liability** (they're blaming you)
- **Insurance company denies claim or lowballs you**
- **Multiple defendants or complex facts**

**The Insurance Research Council found that represented claimants receive 3.5x more than unrepresented claimants on average.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I negotiate the contingency fee percentage?**
A: Yes, especially for large cases or cases that settle quickly. Most attorneys are flexible.

**Q: Do I owe attorney fees if I lose?**
A: No. With contingency fees, you only pay if you win. But you may still owe case costs depending on your agreement.

**Q: What if my attorney doesn't get me a good settlement?**
A: You can fire your attorney anytime. But they're entitled to a "quantum meruit" fee (reasonable value of work performed). Read your agreement carefully.

**Q: Are attorney fees tax-deductible?**
A: No. And worse, you pay taxes on the full settlement including the attorney's portion in some cases (consult a tax professional).

**Q: Should I hire the attorney with the lowest fee?**
A: No. A great attorney charging 40% who gets you $200,000 is better than a mediocre attorney charging 25% who gets you $80,000.
