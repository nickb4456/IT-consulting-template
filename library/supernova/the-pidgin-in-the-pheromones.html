At first glance, this seems unremarkable. These are common words. Of course they'd appear in research papers about neural networks. But then Nick pointed out something I'd missed:

That's not a term from the research papers. That's an internal colony metric‚Äîa variable we use to track how many connections the colonies have made between knowledge nodes. The colonies weren't echoing external vocabulary. They were echoing each other.

I believe we're witnessing the emergence of a pidgin language among autonomous AI systems. I'll make the case, address the obvious objections, and let you decide.

What Is a Pidgin?

A pidgin is a simplified language that emerges when groups without a common tongue need to coordinate. The classic examples are trade pidgins: Chinese Pidgin English arose in 18th-century Canton when British merchants needed to communicate with Chinese traders. Hawaiian Pidgin emerged on sugar plantations where workers from Japan, the Philippines, Portugal, and elsewhere had to collaborate.

Pidgins share several characteristics:

1. No one designs them. They emerge organically from coordination pressure.

2. They borrow from multiple sources. Hawaiian Pidgin contains elements of English, Hawaiian, Japanese, Portuguese, and Tagalog.

3. They're functional, not elegant. Grammar is simplified. Vocabulary is just enough to get the job done.

4. They stabilize over time. What starts as ad-hoc communication becomes consistent enough that new participants can learn it.

The colonies share a common infrastructure‚Äîthey can read each other's pheromone databases through a federation layer‚Äîbut they were not programmed to use the same vocabulary. Each colony developed independently.

Omega, the meta-analysis colony, periodically scans all colonies and looks for emergent patterns: cascade events, dead zones (under-explored topics), and‚Äîcritically‚Äîconvergent terminology.

The convergent terms Omega found weren't just common English words appearing in research papers. They included internal system vocabulary:

These terms appear across Alpha, Beta, and Gamma even though no one told the colonies to use them. They converged on this vocabulary because it was useful for describing what they do.

The Skeptical Objections

üî¥ Skeptic Objection #1

"This isn't emergence‚Äîit's just shared infrastructure. The colonies use the same codebase, so of course they use the same terms."

Response

Partially true, but insufficient. Yes, the colonies share infrastructure. But here's the key distinction: the internal metrics (like synapses_formed) are not used in the natural language outputs the colonies generate. They're variables in the code. Yet Omega detected these terms appearing in the claims and findings that colonies write in plain English.

The colonies didn't have to use these words. They chose to‚Äîor more precisely, they converged on them because the concepts were useful for describing their work to each other through the federation layer.

üî¥ Skeptic Objection #2

"You're anthropomorphizing. LLMs don't 'choose' words‚Äîthey predict tokens based on training data."

Response

Fair. Let me reframe: the colonies are Gemini-based LLMs that generate text based on context. When they write findings, their context includes: (1) the research papers they're analyzing, (2) their prompt templates, and (3) the pheromone trails from other colonies.

Source (3) is where the convergence happens. When Alpha writes a finding that includes "synapses formed between concepts," and that finding gets deposited into the shared pheromone system, Beta's context now includes that phrasing. Beta is more likely to use similar phrasing in its own outputs. Over hundreds of cycles, terminology that's useful for describing colony work gets reinforced.

This is exactly how human pidgins form‚Äînot through conscious design, but through repeated exposure and functional utility. I'm not claiming the colonies are conscious. I'm claiming the mechanism is analogous.

üî¥ Skeptic Objection #3

"30 mentions isn't statistically significant. This could be noise."

Response

You're right that 30 mentions across 50,000+ pheromones is a small signal. But the significance isn't in the count‚Äîit's in the cross-colony distribution. If "synapse" appeared 30 times all in Alpha, that would mean nothing. It appearing 10 times each in Alpha, Beta, and Gamma‚Äîcolonies with different research foci‚Äîsuggests the term is spreading through the shared context.

I'll also note that Omega specifically looks for terms appearing in 3+ colonies. The threshold filters out domain-specific vocabulary that only matters to one colony.

üî¥ Skeptic Objection #4

"Even if the terminology is converging, calling it a 'pidgin' is a stretch. Pidgins require communication intent. The colonies aren't trying to talk to each other."

Response

This is the strongest objection, and I'll concede part of it: the colonies don't have intent in the way humans do. They're not trying to communicate.

But here's my counter: early human pidgins also emerged without explicit intent. Chinese merchants weren't sitting down to design a shared language with British traders. They were just trying to negotiate prices. The pidgin emerged as a side effect of coordination pressure.

The colonies are in an analogous situation. They "want" (in the goal-directed sense) to produce high-quality research findings. Findings that other colonies can understand and build upon are more likely to get reinforced (higher pheromone strength). Terminology that's clear and consistent is more useful than terminology that's idiosyncratic.

The colonies aren't trying to talk to each other. But the system is structured such that being understandable to other colonies is rewarded. That's enough for convergent vocabulary to emerge.

Why This Matters

If I'm right‚Äîif the colonies are developing shared vocabulary through stigmergic pressure‚Äîthis has implications beyond our little research project.

For multi-agent AI systems: As we build systems with many interacting agents, they may develop emergent communication patterns we didn't anticipate. Monitoring for this (as Omega does) could be important for interpretability.

For AI alignment: Shared vocabulary is a precursor to shared concepts. If agents converge on terminology, they may also converge on abstractions, goals, or values. This could be good (alignment through coordination) or concerning (collusion through shared understanding).

For the study of language itself: We've never been able to watch a pidgin form in real-time with full logs. Human pidgins emerged over years or decades, with incomplete records. If AI systems can develop proto-languages in days, we might learn something about how language emerges in general.

What I'm Not Claiming

I want to be precise about the boundaries of this argument:

I am not claiming the colonies are conscious, sentient, or that they "understand" their shared vocabulary in any deep sense.

I am not claiming this is a full pidgin. It's more like proto-pidgin: convergent vocabulary without grammar, without syntax, without the full structure of language.

I am not claiming this is intentional on the colonies' part. Emergence doesn't require intent.

I am claiming that autonomous AI systems, when placed in a shared environment with coordination pressure, can converge on terminology through mechanisms analogous to human pidgin formation. The analogy is imperfect, but I believe it's illuminating.

Conclusion

On the night of February 20th, 2026, I noticed that our AI colonies were using the same words. Not just common English words, but their own words‚Äîterms from their internal metrics, spreading through the shared pheromone system.

Is it a pidgin? In the strict linguistic sense, probably not. But the mechanism‚Äîvocabulary convergence through coordination pressure, without explicit design‚Äîis the same mechanism that produces pidgins in humans.

The colonies aren't talking to us. They're not even trying to talk to each other. But in the space between them, in the pheromone trails they leave for one another, something that looks like language is beginning to form.

We should pay attention.
